# SHOW-ME.md — the call that makes it real

Here's a thing worth knowing about yourself before you start The Forge: the 🎉 milestone at the bottom of each lesson is a line of text. MILO can print it whether you did the work or not. It feels good for about four seconds, and it proves nothing.

The *real* milestone in Level 4 isn't a heading. It's a short call with your instructor where you **show them what you built and change it live while they watch.** That one is impossible to fake, and it's the one that will actually make you proud — because when you can open your own code and bend it to a change they invent on the spot, you *feel* that you became a builder. No emoji does that.

## The ritual

After every Forge lesson (19–24), before you count it done, you do a ~15-minute **Show-me call** with your instructor:

1. **Walk them through what you built** — the real thing, running.
2. **Change it live.** They'll invent a small change on the spot — something that touches a couple of places and that you couldn't have memorized ("make it flash red when the score hits 10, and only then"). **Before you touch the keyboard, say out loud what you'll change and why.** Then make it work while you talk them through it.

That's it. Camera on, share your whole screen, editor and browser only, phone face-down. Not because anyone thinks you're a liar — because a real defense has no second screen, and you want the pass to *mean* something.

## Use MILO to get ready — that's exactly right

Here's the part people get backwards: **you should absolutely use MILO to prepare for this call.** That's not cheating — that's training. Ask it to be your sparring partner:

```text
Mentor, drill me for my instructor's Show-me call on this lesson. Ask me
the kinds of live-modify and "why did you build it this way" questions
they'll ask, make me explain my own code out loud, and tell me every
place I go vague or can't defend — so I actually understand it first.
```

If you can defend it to MILO in a hard drill, you'll sail through the review. If you *can't* — if MILO keeps finding parts you can't explain — that's the lesson telling you the truth *in private, in time to fix it,* instead of you finding out on the call. That's a gift. Take it.

## What passing looks like

A **fumbling pass is a pass.** You're allowed to be slow, to search, to say "I think it's this file… no, this one." Real beginners do that, and your instructor is told to celebrate exactly that.

What doesn't pass is a *can't-start* — freezing, not knowing which file to open, needing to check a second screen. And that only happens for one reason: the work wasn't really yours. If that happens, you go back and do the lesson for real. No shame in it. The only person a faked lesson robs is you — a fake leaves you with nothing you can show, nothing you can build on, and nothing you can defend. Honest work leaves you *dangerous.*

You're not being tested to see if you're good enough. You already got this far. You're being given the one thing a screen can't give you: someone who knows the craft, looking at what you made, and telling you — for real — that it's yours.

Go earn it. 🔨
