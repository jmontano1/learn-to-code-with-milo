# Lesson 16 — Level 3: The Launch Track

You built Study Buddy. You took a real complaint about a real app and turned it into a repro, a hypothesis, a spec, and a working prototype. You shipped a contribution. That was the whole Builder's Track, and you finished it.

So here is a hard truth, and I am going to say it plainly because you have earned plain talk:

**Nobody knows your work exists. And nobody is coming to find it.**

The internet is full of good things nobody ever sees. A brilliant app with zero users loses to a mediocre app everyone has heard of — every single time. The gap between them is not code. It is that someone told the world about one of them, on purpose, and measured whether the world listened.

That is the skill of Level 3. Welcome to the **Launch Track**.

---

## What changes in Level 3

Level 2 made you a builder. Level 3 makes you dangerous.

| | Level 2 (Builder) | Level 3 (Launch) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Who it's for | You, learning | A **real stranger** you have to convince |
| Where it lives | On your laptop | **Live on the public internet**, with a URL |
| "Done" means | It works | A **real human did the thing you asked** |
| The scary part | "I don't know how to build it" | "What if I ship it and nobody cares?" |
| How you win | It runs | You **set a number, chased it, and told the truth** about the result |

Notice the last row. In Level 1 you asked "did it work?" In Level 2 you had a "Prove it" checklist. In Level 3 you pick **one number before you start** and then find out, in public, whether you hit it. That is the most honest kind of work there is. There is nowhere to hide behind "it's basically done."

---

## The one idea of Level 3: make people care, then count

Building is speaking. Launching is being heard.

Everything in this level trains one loop:

**Pick who you're for → make an offer they'd care about → put it in front of them → count what happened → learn from the number.**

That loop is not marketing fluff. It is the same discipline as your capstone — *decide what should happen, then verify it actually did* — pointed at humans instead of code. Engineers who can also do this loop are rare, and they are the ones who get to build the things they want to build.

---

## Two lessons. First a launch, then an engine.

Level 3 is two serious projects, each harder than the last.

**First, the launch: [lessons/17-deploy-a-marketing-campaign.md](17-deploy-a-marketing-campaign.md).** In it you will:

- Pick one real thing to promote and **one real person** to promote it to.
- Choose **one number** you will try to move, with a target and a deadline.
- Build a landing page and **put it live on the internet** where a stranger can open it.
- Write a real campaign — a headline, a message, posts, an email — under tight constraints.
- Launch it, **count the result**, and write an honest retro.

**Then, the engine: [lessons/18-the-growth-engine.md](18-the-growth-engine.md).** Once you've launched *once*, you build the machine that launches, tests, and improves *itself* — advanced AI marketing. You'll use AI to generate at speed, then run a **pre-registered A/B experiment**, count an honest funnel, and prove with real math whether the engine turned. It is the hardest lesson in the repo, on purpose.

Each is harder than the capstone. You will feel, at least once, like you have no idea whether any of it will work. Good. That feeling — shipping anyway, then measuring instead of guessing — is the entire point.

---

## Before you start

Two promises to yourself:

1. **Real humans only.** No fake numbers, no bought followers, no counting yourself. A campaign that honestly reaches two real people beats one that pretends to reach a thousand. Lying to your own metric is the one unforgivable move in Level 3.
2. **The number is the teacher, not the judge.** You might hit your target. You might miss it badly. Either way you *win the lesson*, as long as you measured honestly and can say why it went the way it did. Missing a real target teaches more than hitting a fake one.
