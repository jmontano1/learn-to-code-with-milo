# Lesson 1: How the Web Works 🌐

In the last lesson (`lessons/00-start-here.md`) you toured the repo. Nice work. Now let's understand what a web page actually **is**, before you build one.

Every web page you have ever seen — YouTube, a game site, your school portal — is made of just **three** parts working together. Once you can spot those three parts, the whole web stops feeling like magic and starts feeling like something *you* can build.

Still no typing today. Just one idea at a time.

## The human body analogy

The easiest way to remember the three parts is to picture a human body.

- **HTML is the bones.** Bones give you structure: a head, arms, and legs, all in the right places. HTML (short for "HyperText Markup Language," but you do not need to memorize that) is the structure of a page: the headings, the paragraphs, the buttons. It decides *what* is on the page.

- **CSS is the clothes.** Clothes decide how you *look*: the colors, the style, whether things are plain or bold. CSS decides how the page looks: the colors, the fonts, the spacing. Same bones, different outfit.

- **JavaScript is the muscles.** Muscles make the body *move* and *react*. JavaScript makes the page *do* things. When you click a button and something happens, that is JavaScript.

Notice the order. First you need bones. Then you can dress them. Then they can move. That is exactly the order you will build in.

## Quick reference table

| Part | Body analogy | Its job | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|---------|
| HTML | Bones 🦴 | Structure: *what* is on the page | A heading that says "Hello" |
| CSS | Clothes 👕 | Style: *how* it looks | Making that heading blue |
| JavaScript | Muscles 💪 | Behavior: *what it does* | A button that shows a message when clicked |

Keep this table in your back pocket. For the rest of these lessons, whenever you look at a page, you can quietly ask yourself: "Is this bones, clothes, or muscles?"

## Your task: see all three at once

Let's watch the three parts team up in one tiny example. You will not type anything yet. You are just going to *look*, and let MILO point out which part is which.

Copy this prompt and paste it to MILO in Claude Code:


When MILO's answer comes back, read it slowly and match each labeled part to the table above. You are not memorizing anything. You are just training your eye to spot bones, clothes, and muscles.
