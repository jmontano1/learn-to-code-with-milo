# Lesson 20 — The Content Engine 📣

You know who GetAdvice is for, you have a voice guide, and there's a landing page with your fingerprints on it. But a landing page just sits there and waits — content goes out into the world and *finds* people. Today you build the machine that keeps it going: a 4-week content calendar and your first four posts.

---

## What you'll learn

- The **hook / value / ask** anatomy that every post that works is built on
- How to plan a content calendar you can actually sustain (spoiler: two posts a week beats a heroic sprint)
- How to **batch-draft with MILO** — and why every single draft still gets edited by your hand
- Two formats you'll use over and over: the **short-video script** and the **caption post**
- How to tie every post to exactly **one persona pain**

---

## Why this matters

GetAdvice is launching for real, and launches don't run on one big announcement. They run on a steady drumbeat of small, useful posts that reach people who feel intimidated by AI — one pain at a time, week after week.

Most content efforts die the same death: someone posts every day for six days, burns out, and goes silent for two months. The account that posts twice a week for months wins. **Sustainable beats heroic.** Your job today is to design the sustainable version — and your calendar and drafts are real inputs the launch works from, not homework that disappears into a drawer.

---

## The anatomy: hook, value, ask

Every post you write from now on has three parts, in this order.

Think of it like a street musician. The **hook** is the opening notes that make a stranger stop walking. The **value** is the song — the reason they stay. The **ask** is the open guitar case: a small, obvious next step.

| Part | Job | Rule |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Hook** | Stop the scroll | First line (or first 3 seconds of video). It names the *pain*, not the product. |
| **Value** | Earn the attention | One genuinely useful thing — small enough that they can use it today. |
| **Ask** | Offer a next step | Exactly one: follow, comment, or visit getadviceapp.com. Never two. |

Notice what's missing: there is no "part 4: talk about the app for a while." If your value section is real, the ask feels natural. If it isn't, no amount of asking saves it.

And here is the discipline that separates a content engine from noise: **every post speaks to one persona, about one pain.** Not "people in general." Not "three pains at once." One person, one problem. Your audience research in `marketing/research/audience.md` already did the hard part — today you cash it in.

---

## The calendar: sustainable beats heroic

Four weeks. Two posts per week. Eight posts total.

The cleanest structure: pick **four persona pains**, give each pain **one week**, and hit that week's pain twice — once as a short-video script, once as a caption post. Same pain, two angles. That repetition isn't lazy; it's how a message actually lands.

A **short-video script** is 30 seconds max, written as what you'd say into a phone camera plus `[ON SCREEN: ...]` text cues. A **caption post** is a text post — under 150 words, first line is the hook. The anatomy is identical on every platform, which is why we write the post, not the platform.

## Build it

### Step 1 — [You do] Pick your four pains

Re-read `marketing/research/audience.md`. Write down the four pains you believe most, each as a one-sentence "I" statement in the persona's own words — for example: *"I'm afraid I'll ask the AI something and look stupid."* If you can't say a pain in one sentence, you don't understand it yet — go back to the research, not to your imagination.

### Step 2 — [Ask MILO] Pressure-test the pains

```text
Here are the four persona pains I want my next four weeks of content
to speak to, from marketing/research/audience.md:

1. [pain one]
2. [pain two]
3. [pain three]
4. [pain four]

For each one, ask me WHY this pain would make someone stop scrolling.
If I can't answer in one sentence, help me sharpen the wording — but
don't replace my pains with your own ideas.
```

That "why" is the whole exercise. A pain you can't defend is a post that won't land. **Always ask why** — of MILO, and of yourself.

### Step 3 — [You do] Fill in the calendar

Copy `templates/content-calendar-template.md` to `marketing/content/calendar.md` and fill it in: eight rows, each with a number (01–08), week, format, persona, pain, a rough hook idea, and a status. Then write your four weekly themes and sign the three rules at the bottom. Yes, actually write them out — a rule you typed is harder to break than a rule you skimmed.

### Step 4 — [Ask MILO] Critique the calendar

```text
Here is my 4-week content calendar from marketing/content/calendar.md:

[paste the table]

Check three things: (1) is every post tied to exactly one persona
pain, (2) is two posts a week actually sustainable given what each
format requires, (3) do the four weeks build on each other or just
repeat? Point at specific rows. Ask me why I chose any row that
looks off-theme.
```

Fix what the critique surfaces. Rewriting a calendar row costs you one minute now; discovering week 3 is broken *during* week 3 costs you the week.

### Step 5 — [Ask MILO] Batch-draft the first four posts

Batching is the secret to sustainability: you draft in one focused sitting instead of facing a blank page eight separate times.

```text
We're batch-drafting content for the GetAdvice launch. GetAdvice is a
friendly iOS app that helps everyday people learn and use AI with
confidence (getadviceapp.com).

Here is my voice guide: [paste marketing/voice/voice-guide.md]
Here are rows 01–04 of my calendar: [paste them]

Draft all four posts, one at a time. Each must follow hook / value /
ask, speak to that row's ONE persona pain, and match my voice guide.
Video scripts: 30 seconds max, spoken lines plus [ON SCREEN: ...]
cues. Captions: under 150 words, first line is the hook.

Label every draft "ROUGH — needs my edit." These are clay, not
finished posts.
```

### Step 6 — [You do] Edit every draft by hand

This step is not optional, and it's where the Level 2 rule follows you into Level 3: **write it yourself.** Back then it meant code; now it means your words. MILO hands you clay — you shape it. A post in MILO's default voice sounds like everyone else's AI-drafted post, and your audience can smell it.

Your edit pass, for each draft:

1. Read it out loud. Anywhere you stumble, the reader stumbles.
2. Rewrite the hook **in your own words**, even if MILO's was decent.
3. Cut every line the persona wouldn't care about. Be ruthless.
4. Confirm there is exactly one ask.
5. Check it against your voice guide — preferred words in, banned words out.

### Step 7 — [You do] File the posts

Copy `templates/content-post-template.md` once per post and save them as `marketing/content/posts/NN-slug.md` — `NN` is the two-digit calendar number, and the slug is 2–4 lowercase hyphenated words. Example: `marketing/content/posts/01-afraid-to-ask.md`. Fill in every field, paste your edited draft, and — importantly — fill in the **Edit notes** section: what you changed from MILO's version, and why. That's your proof of hand-editing, and your own record of what your voice actually is.

### Step 8 — [Ask MILO] Final review

```text
Here are my four edited posts: [paste all four]

For each one, check: does the hook name the pain in the first line,
does the value give one genuinely useful thing, and is there exactly
one ask? Then ask me why I made my biggest edit on each — I want to
be able to defend my changes.
```

If you can defend the edits, the words are yours. That's the bar.

---

## Ship it 🚀

Commit your work in two clear pieces, then push:

```bash
git add marketing/content/calendar.md
git commit -m "content: 4-week calendar — two posts/week, one pain per week"

git add marketing/content/posts/
git commit -m "content: first 4 posts drafted with MILO, edited by hand"

git push
```

Then update this week's journal at `marketing/journal/week-NN.md`, starting with the exact front-matter block:

```text
---
week: N
focus: <one line>
shipped: <comma-separated deliverables>
learned: <one line>
blocked: <one line or "nothing">
feedback-for-getadvice: <one line or "none">
---
```

Remember how this team works: **MILO reads these files automatically — the front-matter IS your status report.** Nobody is going to chase you or ask "how's it going?" Your commits and your journal are how the launch knows what you shipped.
